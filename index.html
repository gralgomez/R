Introduction

This project aims to revisit the Solow economic growth model. The approach is based on the findings of the paper written by Mankiw, Romer and Weil 1992. In this paper, Mankiv et al. examine the theoretical implications of the Solow model empirically with a 25 years time span [1960-1985] data set from the Penn World Tables (Summer & Heston 1988). Furthermore they create a so called "augmented Solow model" including human- and physical capital accumulation which are not included in the original Solow model (1956).

This projects recreates the augmented Solow model in R and extends the empirical data to a time span from 1986-2010.

Theory of the model

Original Solow model

The Solowâ€“Swan model is an exogenous growth model, an economic model of long-run economic growth set within the framework of neoclassical economics. It attempts to explain long-run economic growth by looking at a production function (Y(t)) defined by: 1-capital accumulation (k(t)), 2-labor or population growth (L), and 3-increases in productivity, commonly referred to as technological progress (A).

  • Y(t) = K(t)^alfa (A(t) * L(t))^alfa - 1, and (0 < alfa < 1)

Enhanced Solow model

  • ln(Y/L) = alfa + alfa/(1-alfa)ln(s) - alfa/(a-alfa)ln(n + g + delta) + epsilon.

Data and Samples

For the empirical testing of the original Solow model the Penn World Table is used.

Original model variables:

  • Capital accumulation (k(t))
  • Labor or population growth (L)
  • Technological progress (A)

Augmented model variables (+ those of the original model):

  • Human capital accumulation (H) Given by education. In order to obtain this information we followed the Wood (2013) approach: calculate total percentage of secondary school enrollment from UNESCO Education statistics divided by share of working age population.
  • Physical capital accumulation (P)

Further, Mankiw, Romer and Weil (1992) differentiate between three main country groups which are put into comparison:

  • OECD countries: (22 countries) Countries members of the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development.(http://www.oecd.org/)This sample has the advantage that is mostly of high quality and that the variation in omitted country-specific factors is likely to be small. But it has the disadvantages that it is small in size and that it discard much of the variation in the variables of interest.
  • Non-Oil countries: These countries were selected other than Oil countries, mostly because the data was available. Oil countries were excluded since their GDP represents mostly the extraction of existing resources and not value added.
  • Intermediate Economies (IE): (75 countries) Countries with grade of "D" from Summers and Heston (1988) or whose populations in 1960 were less than one million. Summers and Heston use the "D" grade to identify countries whose real income figures are based on extremely little primary data; measurement error is likely to be a greater problem for these countries.

Data Sources:

In order to extract and aggregate the previously mentioned variables according to the model(s) to be tested, following data sources have been used:

  • PWT (Penn World Table) - The Penn World Table (PWT) provides purchasing power parity and national income accounts converted to international prices for 189 countries/territories for some or all of the years 1950-2010. The dataset was collected by Summer & Heston (1988). This dataset represents our main data source.
  • WDI (World Bank data) - The World Bank data is the primary World Bank collection of development indicators, compiled from officially-recognized international sources. It presents the most current and accurate global development data available, and includes national, regional and global estimates. This dataset was used in order to extract the share of working-age population which wasn't represented in the PWT dataset.
  • WRW, TTW (UNESCO data base) - Gross enrollment ratio (pre-primary, primary, lower secondary, upper secondary, tertiary). This information was used to enhance the original Solow Model as the human capital accumulation.

Findings & Conclusions (so far)

  • Model predictions concur with the empirical evidence
  • Construction of an additional variable to account for the impact of human capital formation
  • Test for conditional and unconditional convergence
  • Finding: Omitting human capital accumulation biases the estimated coefficients on saving and population growth
